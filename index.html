Safety Information

Essential Safety Guidelines for Queen Rose Trail

This guide provides essential information to help you stay safe while hiking the Queen Rose Trail. Please familiarize yourself with this information before starting your hike.

Encountering wildlife is a part of the hiking experience. By taking precautions and knowing how to react, you can minimize risks.

General Safety Around Wildlife:

  • Always maintain a respectful distance from all animals.
  • Never feed wildlife. This can make them dependent on humans and may lead to aggressive behavior.
  • Do not try to touch or provoke any animal.
  • Be aware of your surroundings and listen for sounds that might indicate animals are nearby.
  • Store all food securely to prevent attracting animals.

Mpumalanga is home to various snake species. While most are not dangerous, it's important to be cautious.

Prevention:

  • Stay strictly on marked trails.
  • Wear sturdy closed hiking boots and long pants.
  • Watch carefully where you step and where you place your hands, especially when moving over rocks or logs.
  • Avoid walking through thick grass or dense undergrowth whenever possible.
  • If staying overnight in tents, keep them zipped up and check your shoes and gear before use.

During an Encounter:

  • If you see a snake, stop and remain calm. Snakes usually only bite if they feel threatened or are surprised.
  • Slowly back away from the snake, giving it plenty of space to move away. Snakes do not chase people.
  • Do not attempt to handle, corner, or kill the snake.

If Bitten:

  • Stay calm. Panicking can make symptoms worse.
  • Seek immediate medical attention. Contact the emergency number for the trail or local authorities without delay.
  • Keep the bitten limb as still as possible, ideally below the level of the heart.
  • Remove any tight jewelry or clothing near the bite area before swelling begins.
  • Do NOT cut the wound or attempt to suck out the venom.
  • Do NOT apply a tourniquet.
  • Do NOT apply ice or heat to the bite.
  • Try to remember the appearance of the snake (size, color, pattern, head shape) for identification, but only if it is safe to do so and does not delay seeking help.

Baboons are intelligent and can be present in hiking areas. While generally not aggressive towards humans unless provoked or habituated to being fed, their behavior can be unpredictable.

Prevention:

  • Never feed baboons. This is the most common cause of problematic encounters.
  • Keep all food and drinks secured and out of sight in your backpack.
  • Avoid eating or displaying food when baboons are nearby.

During an Encounter:

  • Do not make direct eye contact, as this can be perceived as a challenge.
  • Do not smile, as showing teeth can be interpreted as aggression.
  • Do not run away, as this may trigger a chase instinct.
  • Stand tall and confident. If the baboon approaches, back away slowly without turning your back completely.
  • If a baboon grabs a bag or food item, it may be safer to let it go rather than risk injury trying to retrieve it.
  • Be extra cautious around female baboons with infants.
  • If a baboon displays aggressive signs (such as yawning to show teeth, slapping the ground, or mock charging), back away slowly and deliberately.

Being prepared for unexpected situations can make a significant difference.

Essential Gear:

Always carry a backpack with essential items, even on short day hikes. This includes:

  • Sufficient water and extra food.
  • A basic First Aid kit.
  • A headlamp or flashlight with spare batteries.
  • A knife or multi-tool.
  • Fire-starting materials in a waterproof container.
  • A whistle for signaling for help (the international distress signal is six short blasts).
  • Extra clothing layers, including waterproof and warm layers, regardless of the forecast.
  • Sun protection (sunscreen, hat).
  • Navigation tools: a map and compass are essential backups, even if you are using GPS on your phone. Know how to use them.
  • An emergency blanket or lightweight emergency shelter.
  • A fully charged mobile phone (but be aware that signal may be limited). Consider a power bank.

If You Get Lost:

  • S.T.O.P. - Stop, Think, Observe, Plan. Stay calm.
  • Stay together if you are in a group.
  • Try to retrace your steps carefully if you are absolutely certain of where you last knew your location.
  • If unsure, stay in one place. It's easier for search and rescue teams to find a stationary target than someone who is moving.
  • Use your whistle or other signaling methods to attract attention.
  • Find or create shelter if needed.
  • Stay near the trail if possible, as searchers are likely to follow established paths.

Hypothermia (Dangerously Low Body Temperature):

This can occur even in relatively mild temperatures, especially if you are wet or it is windy.

Prevention:

  • Dress in layers, stay dry by wearing waterproof clothing in rain
  • Avoid prolonged exposure to wind
  • Wear a warm hat as significant body heat is lost through the head

Symptoms:

  • Shivering (may stop in severe cases)
  • Confusion, drowsiness, slurred speech
  • Stumbling, loss of coordination
  • Pale and cold skin

Treatment:

  • Get to a sheltered area immediately
  • Remove any wet clothing and put on dry layers
  • Wrap them in blankets (including an emergency foil blanket)
  • Share body heat with others
  • If conscious, provide warm, non-caffeinated, non-alcoholic drinks
  • Seek medical help

Heat Exposure (Heat Exhaustion and Heatstroke):

High temperatures, especially when combined with humidity and physical exertion, can lead to heat illnesses.

Prevention:

  • Stay Hydrated: Drink water frequently throughout your hike, even before you feel thirsty
  • Wear light-colored, loose-fitting, breathable clothing
  • Wear a wide-brimmed hat and apply sunscreen regularly
  • Plan your hike to avoid the hottest parts of the day (typically late morning to afternoon)
  • Take frequent breaks in shaded areas

Recognizing Heat Exhaustion:

Heavy sweating, pale/clammy skin, muscle cramps, fatigue, weakness, dizziness, headache, nausea or vomiting, fast/weak pulse

Treating Heat Exhaustion:

  • Move to a cool, shaded location
  • Lie down and slightly elevate your legs
  • Drink cool water or an electrolyte drink
  • Loosen any tight clothing
  • Cool down by applying cool, wet cloths to your skin or fanning yourself
  • If symptoms do not improve within an hour or worsen, seek medical attention

Recognizing Heatstroke (A Medical Emergency):

High body temperature (often 40°C or higher), hot/red/dry or sometimes damp skin, confusion, disorientation, slurred speech, nausea, vomiting, rapid/strong pulse, may lose consciousness, lack of sweating in some cases.

Treating Heatstroke (While Waiting for Help):

  • Call for emergency help immediately. Heatstroke is life-threatening
  • Move the person to the coolest possible area immediately
  • Begin cooling the person down urgently using any means available
  • Do NOT give fluids if the person is unconscious

Recognizing and Responding to Imminent Lightning Storm Threat

1. Signs of an Approaching Storm:

Be vigilant for these indicators:

  • Darkening skies: Especially large, towering cumulonimbus clouds.
  • Distant thunder: If you can hear thunder, you are within striking distance of lightning. The "30/30 Rule" is a critical guideline: If the time between seeing lightning and hearing thunder is 30 seconds or less, seek shelter immediately. Wait 30 minutes after the last thunder is heard before resuming activity.
  • Increasing wind: Often a precursor to storms.
  • Sudden drop in temperature.
  • Static electricity: Hair standing on end, tingling skin, or a metallic taste in your mouth indicate an imminent strike. Immediately assume the lightning-safe position.

2. Immediate Actions Upon Threat Recognition:

  • Descend immediately: Your primary goal is to get off high, exposed ground (summits, ridges, exposed passes). Aim for lower elevations.
  • Avoid open areas: Do not stand in open fields or on a wide-open plateau.
  • Seek safe ground:
    • Dense forest: Provides some protection from direct strikes, but still carries risk. Disperse groups by at least 20 feet.
    • Deep valleys or ravines: Offer better protection than high ground.
    • Large, permanent shelters: If available (e.g., stone huts, substantial buildings), but avoid small, flimsy shelters like open-sided picnic shelters.
  • Avoid water: Lakes, rivers, and wet ground conduct electricity.
  • Avoid lone trees or small groves: Lightning often strikes the tallest object.
  • Avoid metal objects: Put down trekking poles, ice axes, and remove anything metal that could attract a strike (e.g., jewelry, backpack frames).
  • Disperse from group: If in a group, spread out by at least 20-30 feet. This prevents a single strike from affecting everyone.

3. Lightning-Safe Position

If a direct strike is imminent, or you cannot reach better shelter, adopt the lightning-safe position:

  • Crouch low to the ground: With feet together, head tucked, and hands over your ears.
  • Minimize ground contact: Sit on your backpack or a foam pad if possible to insulate yourself from ground currents.
  • Do NOT lie flat: This increases your contact with the ground and potential exposure to ground currents.

4. Post-Strike Protocol

4.1 First Aid:

  • Assess the scene: Ensure it is safe before approaching a victim. Lightning victims do not carry an electrical charge.
  • Check for responsiveness: Call for help.
  • Administer CPR: If the victim is not breathing and has no pulse. Lightning can cause cardiac arrest.
  • Treat for burns: Lightning can cause severe burns.
  • Treat for shock.

4.2 Communication:

  • Call emergency services immediately e.g., 112 in South Africa. Provide your exact location and the nature of the emergency.

5. Education and Training

5.1 Continuous Learning:
Hikers should continuously educate themselves on mountain weather patterns and lightning safety through reputable sources (e.g., meteorological services, mountain rescue organizations, outdoor education centers).

5.2 Practice:
Familiarize yourself with safe practices through training and simulated scenarios.

This standard serves as a critical guide. While it provides comprehensive best practices, individual judgment and experience remain paramount in dynamic mountain environments.

Flooded Rivers:

  • Do not attempt to cross rivers that are swollen or flowing rapidly due to rain
  • Wait for the water level to drop, which can happen quickly after rain stops
  • If the water level does not drop or the current is too strong, find an alternative route or turn back

Fire Safety:

  • Respect all rules regarding fires on the trail
  • Only make fires in designated areas if permitted
  • Ensure fires are completely extinguished before leaving
  • Be aware of dry conditions where veld fires can easily start and spread quickly

About Queens River Adventure Camp

Get lost in the mystic Nelshoogte Forest Reserve… Tucked away just 30 km from Barberton, the Queens River Adventure Camp plays host to the Queen Rose Hiking Trails. Easily considered as one of the most scenic trails in the region, several trails start at Queens base camp and leads hikers on an amazing route that traverses over river crossings, pools and wooden bridges, through pine and natural forests.

Bird life is in abundance and you have the opportunity to spot one of the 350 plus bird species. Although some degree of fitness is needed to navigate the forest terrain, the tranquillity and sublime relaxation experienced is therapeutic. Your body can do anything, it’s the mind that needs convincing…

Feel completely safe at Queen Rose, all our hikers are issued with a two-way radio that is monitored at Queens Base Camp. The beauty of our trails is that anyone can do it, as long as you have a reasonable level of fitness and a pair of sturdy hiking boots. Some of the climbs may leave you a little short of breath but the views from the top will certainly take your breath away!

Our hiking trails have it all, the spectacular views, incredible wildlife, entertaining river crossings and perfectly placed natural splash pools and waterfalls. What’s more, anyone can enjoy it from babies in backpacks to senior citizens. Yet the real beauty of the trails is that it enables you to slip away from the stresses of modern-day life and reconnect with nature.

If you should experience any medical issues a vehicle will be dispatched to pick you up where possible. If you are unfit and do not feel capable to finish the trail, don’t stress for a small fee we will send a vehicle to collect you. Slack-packing is available on all trails.

Essential Checklist

This checklist is a guide. Adjust items based on your specific route (day hike vs. multi-day), weather forecast, and personal needs.

GPS Device/App: Optional but useful, our routes are properly marked. Ensure offline maps are downloaded and battery is full (carry a power bank).

Route Description: Printout or digital copy of the trail description/notes.

Water Bottles/Hydration Reservoir: Carry sufficient water. General rule: 1-2 litres per person per half-day of hiking, but this varies greatly with heat and effort.

Water Purification: Filter, purification tablets, or drops. Know the location of reliable water sources on your route if planning to refill.

Trail Snacks: High-energy, easy-to-eat snacks (nuts, dried fruit, energy bars, jerky). Pack more than you think you'll need.

Lunch (for longer hikes): Sandwiches, wraps, or other non-perishable options.

Extra Day's Food: In case of delays.

Hiking Boots/Shoes: Sturdy, broken-in footwear suitable for varied terrain, including potential wet areas. Ankle support is recommended for steeper sections.

Hiking Socks: Moisture-wicking synthetic or wool socks (bring spares). Consider a liner sock to prevent blisters.

Moisture-Wicking Base Layers: (Synthetic or wool) to keep sweat away from your skin.

Mid-Layer: Fleece or insulated jacket for warmth.

Outer Layer: Waterproof and windproof jacket (even in good weather, conditions can change).

Hiking Pants/Shorts: Quick-drying material. Zip-off pants offer versatility.

Sun Hat/Cap: Wide-brimmed hat offers best protection.

Warm Hat/Beanie: Evenings can be cool, especially at higher elevations.

Gloves: If cooler weather is expected.

Sunscreen: High SPF, apply generously and reapply throughout the day, especially after river crossings.

Lip Balm with SPF.

Sunglasses: With UV protection.

Basic First-Aid Kit: Include plasters, antiseptic wipes, pain relievers, blister treatment (moleskin or Compeed), bandages, medical tape, and any personal medications.

Emergency Whistle: For signaling for help.

Headlamp/Torch: With extra batteries (essential, even for day hikes, in case you're delayed).

Fire Starter: Waterproof matches or lighter and tinder (for emergency only).

Knife or Multi-tool.

Emergency Shelter: Lightweight space blanket or emergency bivy (optional but recommended).

Personal ID and Emergency Contacts: Keep on your person. Inform someone of your route and expected return time.

Two-Way Radio: Queen Rose trail provides these and monitors them – understand how to use yours.

Small Repair Kit: Duct tape, safety pins, cordage.

Trekking Poles: Optional, but helpful for stability on varied terrain and steep descents (like Roland's Staircase).

Hand Sanitizer.

Toilet Paper: In a waterproof bag.

Small Trowel: To bury human waste away from water sources.

Biodegradable Soap: For washing hands or dishes (use sparingly and away from water).

Sealable Bag: To pack out all waste, including toilet paper.

Cash: For emergencies or unexpected needs.

Camera: For capturing memories (optional).

Notebook and Pen: For journaling or notes.

Permits/Reservations: If required for your specific trail.

Backpack: Appropriate size for the length of your hike.

Backpack Rain Cover or Waterproof Liner.

Power Bank/Portable Charger: For electronic devices.

Trash Bag: Pack out everything you pack in.

Insect Repellent: Especially during warmer months.

Basic Hiking First Aid Guide (Queen Rose Trail)

This guide is for basic situations. For serious injuries or emergencies, use your provided two-way radio or phone (if signal is available) to contact Queen Rose Base Camp or emergency services immediately. Knowing basic first aid can help manage a situation until professional help arrives.

1. Assessing the Situation:

STOP: Before rushing in, stop and assess the scene for any dangers to yourself or the injured person (e.g., unstable ground, wildlife).

Assess the Person: Check for consciousness, breathing, and severe bleeding. Ask them what happened and where they feel pain.

  • Stay Calm: Panic makes things worse.
  • Assess the Situation: What happened? Where are you? Who is injured and how severely?
  • Use the Radio: Activate your provided two-way radio to contact Queen Rose Base Camp. Clearly state your location (use trail markers or landmarks), the nature of the emergency, and the condition of the injured person. Follow their instructions.
  • Use Your Phone: If you have signal and cannot reach Base Camp via radio, try calling emergency services (112 or other relevant local numbers you should have pre-programmed). Provide your location details.
  • Signal for Help: If you cannot communicate electronically, use a whistle (six short blasts, repeated) or mirror/shiny object to signal aircraft.
  • Stay Put (if necessary): If the injured person cannot move, stay with them. Send for help if there are enough people in your group (ideally send two people, leaving one with the injured, and mark the location on a map for them).

Remember: Prevention is key. Hike within your abilities, wear appropriate gear, stay hydrated and fuelled, and be aware of your surroundings.

2. Common Hiking Issues & Basic Care:

Prevention: Wear well-fitting, broken-in hiking boots and moisture-wicking socks. Address "hot spots" (areas of friction) immediately by covering them with medical tape or moleskin.

Treatment: For intact blisters, clean the area and cover with a blister plaster or moleskin donut to reduce pressure. If a blister is large, painful, or likely to pop on its own, you can drain it: clean the area and a needle with antiseptic, carefully pierce the edge, gently press out the fluid, and cover with a sterile dressing, keeping the 'roof' of the blister intact if possible. Monitor for infection.

Treatment: Clean the wound with antiseptic wipes or clean water. Apply pressure with a clean cloth or gauze to stop bleeding. Once bleeding stops, apply antiseptic ointment and cover with a plaster or sterile dressing.

Treatment (RICE):

  • Rest: Stop activity and avoid putting weight on the injured limb.
  • Ice: Apply a cold pack (or a cloth soaked in cold stream water) to the injured area for 15-20 minutes at a time to reduce swelling and pain.
  • Compression: Gently wrap the injured area with an elastic bandage to help reduce swelling. Ensure it's not too tight (check for numbness, tingling, or increased pain).
  • Elevation: Keep the injured limb raised above heart level if possible.

If severe pain, inability to bear weight, or deformity is present, suspect a fracture and seek immediate medical help. Use trekking poles or sticks to create a makeshift splint if necessary to immobilize the limb.

Treatment: If a stinger is present (like from a bee), scrape it out carefully with a fingernail or credit card edge (avoid squeezing). Clean the area. Apply an antihistamine cream to reduce itching and swelling. Monitor for signs of a severe allergic reaction (difficulty breathing, swelling of face/throat, rash spreading rapidly) and use an EpiPen if the person has one and you are trained, then seek immediate emergency help.

Emergency Response - Quick Guide

This is a basic guide. For full training, take an accredited First Aid and CPR course.

This is based on current guidelines for lay rescuers performing CPR on adults.

Remember: Any attempt at CPR is better than no attempt. Hands-only CPR is recommended for untrained bystanders.

1. Check for Danger:

Before approaching, ensure the scene is safe for you and the person.

2. Check for Response:

Tap the person's shoulder and shout loudly, "Are you O.K.?"

If no response, call for help immediately (shout for someone to call for help - and get an AED if available). If alone, call emergency services yourself using speakerphone.

3. Check for Breathing:

Open the airway by tilting the head back and lifting the chin.

Look, listen, and feel for normal breathing for no more than 10 seconds. Occasional gasps are NOT normal breathing.

4. If No Normal Breathing and Unresponsive, Start CPR:

Chest Compressions:

  • Ensure the person is lying on their back on a firm surface.
  • Kneel beside the person.
  • Place the heel of one hand in the center of the chest, on the lower half of the breastbone.
  • Place your other hand on top of the first hand and interlock your fingers, keeping them off the chest.
  • Position yourself with your shoulders directly over your hands and keep your arms straight.
  • Push down hard and fast, compressing the chest about 5-6 cm (2-2.4 inches).
  • Allow the chest to fully recoil after each compression.
  • Perform compressions at a rate of 100-120 per minute.

Rescue Breaths (If trained and willing):

  • After 30 compressions, open the airway again using the head-tilt, chin-lift.
  • Pinch the soft part of the nose closed.
  • Take a normal breath, seal your mouth over the person's mouth, and blow steadily for about 1 second, watching for the chest to rise.
  • If the chest rises, give a second breath. If it doesn't rise, re-tilt the head and try again. Limit breaths to two attempts to minimize interruptions to compressions.

CPR Cycle:

  • Continue cycles of 30 chest compressions followed by 2 rescue breaths (30:2).

Hands-Only CPR:

  • If you are not trained in rescue breaths or are unwilling, provide continuous chest compressions at a rate of 100-120 per minute.

Continue CPR until:

  • Emergency medical services arrive and take over.
  • The person shows signs of life (moves, breathes normally, coughs, etc.).
  • An AED is available and ready to use.
  • You are too exhausted to continue.

Important Considerations:

  • This is a brief guide and not a substitute for certified training.
  • We recommend users take a full first aid and CPR course.
  • Remember to call emergency services as the absolute first step after checking for response and breathing.

Burns are injuries to the skin caused by heat, chemicals, electricity, or radiation (like the sun). In a hiking environment, common causes can include campfires, stoves, hot liquids, or prolonged sun exposure.

Initial Treatment for Burns:

  • Stop the Burning Process: Remove the person from the source of the burn. If clothing is on fire, stop, drop, and roll, or smother the flames with a blanket or jacket.
  • Cool the Burn: Immediately cool the burned area with cool (not ice-cold) running water for at least 10-20 minutes. If running water isn't available, use cool compresses or immerse the area in cool water. Do NOT use ice, butter, grease, or powders, as these can worsen the injury.
  • Remove Tight Items: Gently remove any jewelry, belts, or tight clothing from around the burned area before swelling occurs, unless they are stuck to the skin.
  • Cover the Burn: Loosely cover the cooled burn with a clean, non-stick dressing or sterile bandage. For minor burns, cling film can be used, but do not wrap it tightly. This helps protect the burn from infection and reduces pain by keeping air off the injured area.
  • Manage Pain: Over-the-counter pain relievers like ibuprofen or paracetamol can help ease pain and reduce inflammation.
  • Seek Medical Attention:
    • For minor burns (redness, pain, possibly small blisters - affecting only the top layer or slightly deeper), keep the area clean and covered. Monitor for signs of infection (increased redness, swelling, pus, fever).
    • For more severe burns (larger blisters, deeper damage, leathery appearance, or burns to the face, hands, feet, genitals, or major joints), or if you are unsure of the severity, seek immediate medical attention. Evacuate the person if necessary and possible, or call for emergency help.
    • Chemical or electrical burns always require immediate medical attention. For chemical burns, flush the area with large amounts of water while carefully removing contaminated clothing.

Do NOT break blisters. They provide a natural barrier against infection. If a blister breaks on its own, gently clean the area and apply a clean dressing.

While uncommon, encounters with snakes are possible. If bitten:

  • Stay Calm: Panic increases venom spread. Reassure the person.
  • Limit Movement: Keep the bitten limb still and below heart level if possible.
  • Remove Constricting Items: Rings, watches, or tight clothing on the affected limb should be removed before swelling occurs.
  • Clean the Bite Area: Gently clean the wound with water or antiseptic wipe if available.
  • DO NOT: Cut the wound, suck out venom, apply a tourniquet, apply ice or heat, or give the person alcohol. These actions are harmful.
  • Seek Immediate Medical Help: Use your provided two-way radio or phone to contact Queen Rose Base Camp or emergency services immediately. Describe the snake if possible (size, colour, markings) without trying to catch or kill it, as identification is less critical than rapid medical assessment and treatment.

Heat Exhaustion: (Symptoms: heavy sweating, paleness, muscle cramps, tiredness, weakness, dizziness, headache, nausea, fainting)

Treatment: Move the person to a cool, shaded area. Have them lie down with their feet slightly raised. Loosen tight clothing. Give sips of cool water. Fan the person. If symptoms worsen or don't improve within an hour, or if vomiting occurs, seek immediate medical help (could be progressing to heatstroke).

Heatstroke: (Symptoms: high body temperature, red/hot/dry or moist skin, confusion, throbbing headache, dizziness, nausea, rapid strong pulse, possibly unconsciousness - this is a medical emergency!)

Treatment: Call for emergency help immediately. Move the person to the coolest possible area. Cool them rapidly using any means available (soak clothing with water, apply wet cloths, fan them). Do NOT give fluids if they are unconscious or confused.

Treatment: Move the person to a warm, dry place. Gently remove wet clothing and replace with dry layers. Wrap them in blankets (including an emergency foil blanket). Dehydration is a state where your body loses more fluid than you take in, disrupting essential functions. When hiking, increased physical exertion, especially in warm or dry conditions or at altitude, leads to increased sweating and fluid loss, making hikers particularly susceptible. Ignoring the signs of dehydration can quickly turn an enjoyable hike into a serious situation.

Recognizing Dehydration:

Early recognition is key. Be aware of the following symptoms:

  • Increased thirst and dry mouth
  • Fatigue or tiredness
  • Headache
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness
  • Dark-colored, strong-smelling urine, or decreased urination
  • Muscle cramps

As dehydration worsens, symptoms can become more severe and may include:

  • Nausea
  • Confusion or disorientation
  • Rapid heart rate and breathing
  • Sunken eyes
  • Lack of sweating
  • Pale or dry skin
  • In severe cases, inability to keep fluids down, or loss of consciousness. Severe dehydration is a medical emergency.

Trailside Remedy:

If you or a hiking companion show signs of dehydration, stop immediately.

  • Find Shade and Rest: Move to a cool, shaded area and cease all physical activity to prevent further fluid loss through sweating.
  • Rehydrate: Sip water slowly. Avoid chugging large amounts, which can lead to nausea. If available, use an electrolyte solution or add an electrolyte tablet or powder to your water. These help replenish vital salts lost through sweat, aiding in faster rehydration.
  • Consume Salty Snacks: Eating salty snacks can also help replace lost electrolytes.
  • Monitor Symptoms: Rest and continue to rehydrate. Do not resume hiking until symptoms significantly improve.
  • Severe Dehydration: If symptoms are severe (confusion, inability to drink, unconsciousness), this is a medical emergency. Seek immediate professional medical help. If in a remote location, be prepared to call for emergency assistance if possible, and administer first aid to the best of your ability while awaiting help.

Prevention is the Best Medicine:

  • Hydrate Before You Hike: Drink plenty of water in the hours leading up to your hike.
  • Drink Regularly During the Hike: Don't wait until you feel thirsty. Sip water consistently, especially during strenuous parts of the trail or in warm weather. Aim for about 0.5 to 1 liter of water per hour of hiking, adjusting based on conditions and exertion level.
  • Use Electrolytes: For longer or more challenging hikes, incorporate electrolyte drinks or snacks.
  • Monitor Urine Color: Pale yellow urine indicates good hydration; dark urine means you need to drink more.
  • Avoid Dehydrating Beverages: Limit caffeine and alcohol before and during your hike.
  • Familiarize yourself with the contents of your first aid kit before your hike.
  • Know how to use the items inside.
  • Keep your kit easily accessible in your backpack.
